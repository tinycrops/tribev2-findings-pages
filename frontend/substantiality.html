In-silico neuroscience · TRIBE v2

When you teach an AI to draw cats, what happens inside its “brain”?

And — the question this whole page is about — is that change substantial: real, repeatable, and meaning what we think it means? Or just one lucky example?

A walkthrough for readers who have never heard of TRIBE v2 · GB10 / Blackwell · 2026-06-15 · part of the tribev2-research project

First, what is TRIBE v2?

Imagine a machine that you can show a video, play a sound, or hand a piece of text — and it predicts which parts of a human brain would light up in response. Not metaphorically: it outputs activity at 20,484 specific points spread across the cortex, the same surface neuroscientists use to read real fMRI scans. That machine is TRIBE v2, an open model from Meta.

How it works. Three frozen “encoders” turn what-you-see, what-you-hear, and what-you-read into numbers; TRIBE v2 fuses them and predicts a brain map. Crucially the text encoder is an ordinary language model — Llama-3.2-3B — used only as a feature extractor. That is the one piece we tampered with.

Why is this useful? It lets you do neuroscience in silico — feed the model carefully controlled stimuli and watch where its predicted brain map responds, with no scanner and no human subject. As a sanity check it already works: play it speech and the predicted map lights up auditory cortex (the brain's hearing region) with no training for that task.

The thing we changed: an “ASCII-cat” finetune

We took the text encoder (Llama) and gave it a small, cheap finetune (a LoRA) on a dataset of cats drawn in ASCII art — pictures made entirely of keyboard characters, like this real one from the apehex/ascii-art dataset we trained and tested on:

Then we fed ASCII art straight into the text encoder as text and asked: does the finetune change where TRIBE predicts the brain lights up? The single most striking early result was that an iconic hand-drawn ASCII cat, after the finetune, pushed the predicted map toward visual cortex (the brain's seeing region) — as if the model now “saw a picture” where before it saw odd text.

Each picture is a human cortex seen from the left and right; warm color = TRIBE predicts more activity there. The back of the brain (right side of each map) is visual cortex. But this is a single example. Exciting hints from one example are exactly what fool you.

The real question: is it substantial?

A finding is substantial when it survives contact with many examples and a control — when it is a property of the phenomenon, not of one lucky stimulus. So we scaled the one-cat hint up to 86 different hand-drawn ASCII animals and added the obvious control: if this is really about cats, cats should move more than dogs and horses.

The twist: substantial, but not about cats generic

Here is where the control matters. If the finetune taught the model something about cats, cats should move more than dogs and horses. They don’t:

Dogs move exactly as much as cats (gray dot = before, colored dot = after). Cats +1.23, dogs +1.24, horses +0.68. The cat-vs-others advantage is just +0.18 — statistically indistinguishable from zero (Mann-Whitney U one-sided p= 0.3002).
What the finetune actually learned. Not “cat.” It learned “this block of glyphs is a picture.” When you feed it the line-drawn ASCII style it was trained on, it routes the input toward visual cortex — regardless of whether the drawing is a cat, a dog, or a horse. The effect is real and rock-solid; it is just about style, not meaning.

That also resolves an earlier mistake of ours. When we first scaled up using photo-to-ASCII conversions (blurry brightness-ramp text, not crisp line art), the effect vanished and we wrote it off as “refuted.” Wrong on both counts: the right way to test a hint is to scale it up in its own regime — same kind of stimulus, plus a control. Do that, and the picture is clean:

Why this matters for what we’re building

This project sits under a larger goal — the ASCII Tribe vision: a model that treats an ASCII drawing not as decorative text or a low-grade photo, but simultaneously as a grid of characters, a visual composition, and a depiction of real things — a world it can perceive and act in.

The vision’s working term for the property we care about is Platonic coherence: the degree to which a concept survives changes in representation, resolution, and rendering. The substantiality test is a first, honest instrument reading on it:

  • Coherence of style is already there. “This is a line-art ASCII picture” transfers cleanly into a visual-cortex signal, 100% of the time. The bridge between character-grid and image exists.
  • Coherence of identity is not — yet. “This particular thing is a cat” does not survive the trip: cat, dog, and horse are indistinguishable. Getting identity to travel with the picture is the open problem.
  • The instruments are not ground truth. The vision is explicit that TRIBE is an instrument, to be calibrated against controls and human judgment — which is exactly why a single iconic cat (Result 2) could over-promise, and why scaling-with-a-control (this test) is the load-bearing step. This is milestone 1, the perceptual substrate: establishing what the encoders actually perceive across character, rendered, and image form before building anything on top.

Vision document: ascii-tribe-lab/VISION.md (the path tribe-local/VISION.md in the request does not exist — this is the canonical one).

The honesty note

A single in-distribution example over-promised. A distribution-shifted scale-up under-claimed. Only scaling within the probe’s own regime, with a matched control, gave the true answer: a real, 100%-consistent effect — for the wrong reason. The finetune is a line-art-ASCII style detector, which is itself the deeper lesson repeated across this project: TRIBE’s predicted brain maps are sensitive to the encoder’s distribution and style, not to its semantics.
